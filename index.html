Ride the East Bay Bike Path

Start of East Bay Bike Path  Mile 0 

The East Bay Bike Path is a scenic, paved 14.5-mile trail running from Providence's India Point Park through East Providence, Barrington, and Warren to Bristol, offering stunning Narragansett Bay views, coastal marshes, parks (like Colt State Park), and historic spots, ideal for cycling, walking, and connecting communities with largely flat, accessible terrain. From Providence: Starts with a scenic downhill run along the waterfront from India Point Park, crossing bridges over rivers. Through East Providence: Features waterfront sections, tunnels, and access to parks like Squantum Woods. Beyond: Continues through Barrington and Warren, eventually reaching Bristol's historic waterfront.

Linear Park  Mile 0.8 

The George Redman Linear Park, named after East Bay Bike Path advocate, traverses on the Washington bridge and is made up of a walking path and a bike path that runs parallel to the I-95 bridge. You can enjoy the view as you start your ride from India Point Park onto the East Bay Bike Path towards Bristol, RI.

India Point Park in Providence is the official start/end point of the popular 14.5-mile East Bay Bike Path, connecting Providence to Bristol crossing the Washington Bridge over the Seekonk River w land running along the waterfront through East Providence, Barrington, and Warren.

Providence, Warren & Bristol Railroad  Mile 2.5 

The Providence, Warren & Bristol (P, W&B) Railroad ran for about 90 years serving as a key line for passengers and freight before closing, with its route later transformed into the East Bay Bike Path. The path was developed between 1987 to 1992. In East Providence, the path drops down a steep embankment to the old railroad corridor spanning Watchemoket Cove on a causeway, where the railroad tracks are often visible.

Pomham Rocks Lighthouse along Rhode Island’s East Bay Bike Path the historic structure dating back to 1871. Pomham Rocks Lighthouse sits atop a tiny rock island that juts out of the waters on Providence River. The lighthouse was built in 1871 to guide mariners around these rocky islands and shorelines.

Watchemoket Cove  Mile 3.5 

Heading south along a split rail fence for the next 1.1 miles you can see unobstructed views of the Providence skyline and shipping facilities. From here, passing by the Squantum Association, and up ahead at Squantum Woods Park, where some 90 bird species have been recorded.

The Squantum Association historic grounds and architectural gems were chartered in 1872 and are located with breathtaking views of Narragansett Bay.

Historic Riverside Square  Mile 4 

Riverside Square in Riverside, East Providence, RI, is a revitalized hub along the East Bay Bike Path, transforming from an old rail line into a pocket neighborhood, green spaces, connecting visitors to Providence and further south via the path, featuring public art, and local dining spots.

The historic train depot has been at the heart of Riverside community for more than 150 years. Borealis Cafe restored the depot into a cafe the buzzes with locals and cyclists. It's and ideal spot along the path for anyone in need of a coffee break to get the gears going.

Riverside  Mile 5 

The next mile of scenery includes water views, wooded sections, and proximity to Narragansett Bay before you reach Crescent View Avenue. Here you can continue to the Haines Memorial State Park or take a trip to the Crescent Park Looff Carousel.

Great Egrets

Frequently Great Egrets and other herons along Rhode Island's East Bay Bike Path are seen especially in marshy areas looking for fish, frogs, and small creatures in the shallows from spring to fall.

The Crescent Park Looff Carousel. an 1895 amusement ride that features 62 carved figures and four chariots. It’s listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

Barrington River Bridge  Mile 9.2 

The trail veers away from the bay for 6 miles as it passes through Barrington; the bridges crossing Barrington and Palmer Rivers. It is especially beautiful with foliage turning the wooded sections vibrant, providing a ride with maritime sights.

Sweet Pepperbush is blooming in Rhode Island’s forest. Nature’s perfume! Look for it on trails that skirt wetland edges, like at Carter Preserve, Tillinghast Pond or King Preserve.

Barrington Palmer Bridge  Mile 10 

The East Bay Bike Path through Barrington offers scenic coastal views, weaving past wetlands, quiet residential areas, and the serene One Hundred Acre Cove, a tidal estuary with marshlands and birdlife.

Hundred Acre Cove in Barrington, RI, is a vital, shallow salt marsh estuary, part of the Barrington River system, known for rich wildlife like diamondback terrapins, various birds (clapper rail, terns), and fish (striped bass), offering scenic kayaking, fishing, and nature viewing.

Wild phlox, also known as meadow phlox or wild sweet William, grows freely in meadows near woodlands and shady roadsides. They come in a range of pale pinks and purples, grow about 2 feet tall, and provide lovely delicate color in the spring countryside.

Warren at Child Street  Mile 10.4 

Cyclists on the East Bay Pathnlove stopping at the Dels Lemonade in Warren, known for its trailside takeout window, classic frozen lemonade, making it a perfect spot for a refreshing break with easy access to bike-friendly treats. Warren’s eclectic Historic Waterfront District is seeing a renaissance of its own driven by a walkable downtown, numerous creative restaurants and food driven businesses out of Hope & Main Food Incubator and the vibrant and arts scene.

Burr's Hill Park in Warren offers stunning water views, hosting community events like concerts at its bandshell, and providing access to the East Bay Bike Path.

BRISTOL  Mile 12 

The East Bay Bike Path in Bristol offers stunning coastal views, featuring birding opportunities, especially near the Audubon Nature Center stop where you can walk a boardwalk for bay vistas. Excellent flat, paved trail for biking, walking, and taking in the seaside New England seasonal marsh smells and walk through the tall grasses known as phragmites.

I

Red-winged blackbirds

are very common along the path, especially in marshy areas, where they perch on and nest in dense invasive Phragmites (common reed) using the tall stalks for cover and breeding, announcing spring with their loud song.

The fields, woods, wetlands and winding boardwalk to the shore of Narragansett Bay present visitors to the Claire D. McIntosh Wildlife Refuge with a wide variety of habitats for nature study and observation. This property is also home to Audubon's award-winning Nature Center and Aquarium, where walks, shore programs, lectures and family events are offered year-round.

Bristol  Mile 13.8 

Along this stretch you can catch glimpses of Narragansett Bay including the Conimicut Lighthouse. After crossing the entrance road to Colt State Park the bike path passes Mill Pond to the right where you are likely to catch glimpses of cormorants and egrets. After passing Poppasquash Road. Poppasquash Neck was primarily farmland within the larger peninsula of Bristol, Rhode Island. Bristol is known for its Revolutionary War history and later development by figures like Samuel Colt.

I

Ospreys

Ospreys are abundant and easily spotted in Warren and Bristol as they thrive in the bay's shallow waters, using both natural and man-made structures for their nests.

Colt State Park's beauty lies in its stunning blend of manicured estates and wild waterfront, featuring expansive green lawns, historic stone walls, and panoramic Narragansett Bay views.

Bristol at Narragansett Bay  Mile 14.5 

The East Bay Bike Path ends along Bristol Harbor. Arriving into the town center brings you to Independence Park, where the path officially terminates at Thames & Oliver Streets. Bristol is an historic New England seaside town known for its well-preserved 18th/19th-century downtown, vibrant waterfront, shipbuilding heritage (Herreshoff Marine Museum), and America's Oldest Fourth of July Parade.
